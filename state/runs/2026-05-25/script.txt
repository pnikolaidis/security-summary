Story one. A critical SQL injection vulnerability in Ghost CMS, tracked as CVE-2026-26980, is being exploited in what researchers describe as a large-scale ClickFix campaign. Ghost is a popular open-source content management system used by blogs, newsletters, and media sites around the world. Attackers are exploiting the SQL injection flaw to inject malicious JavaScript directly into Ghost-powered sites. When visitors arrive, they're shown fake error prompts—styled to look like legitimate system messages—that trick them into copying and pasting attacker-controlled commands into their own computer's terminal. This is the ClickFix technique: weaponizing the user's clipboard against them. Why it matters: if your organization runs a Ghost site, patching is urgent. For end users, any time a website asks you to paste something into your terminal or command prompt, that's a major red flag—stop and investigate before you do anything.

Story two. North Korea's Lazarus Group has a new tool: a cross-platform, memory-only remote access tool called RemotePE. Researchers at Fox-IT documented a multi-stage attack chain that uses two loader programs called DPAPILoader and RemotePELoader to decrypt and deploy the final implant entirely in RAM. Because RemotePE never touches the disk, it leaves no files behind for traditional endpoint detection tools to find, and forensic investigators won't recover artifacts from an infected machine after the fact. Lazarus is targeting financial institutions and cryptocurrency firms—which is entirely consistent with the group's long-running mission of funding North Korean government operations through theft of digital assets. Why it matters: if you work in fintech or crypto, assume this group has you in their sights, and know that conventional file-scanning detection won't catch this one. Behavioral and memory-based monitoring is essential.

Story three. A coordinated supply chain attack called TrapDoor has planted over 34 malicious packages across more than 384 versions on npm, PyPI, and Crates.io—the three dominant package registries for JavaScript, Python, and Rust respectively. The campaign began around May 22nd with waves of packages published from a cluster of accounts all controlled by the same threat actor. The malware payload is designed to steal credentials. Because modern software development relies so heavily on automatic dependency resolution, any developer or automated build pipeline that installed packages from these ecosystems after May 22nd could be affected. Why it matters: audit your recently added dependencies immediately, check them against published indicators of compromise for TrapDoor, and rotate credentials on any systems that ran builds over the last few days.

Bulletproof hosters are companies that rent infrastructure to criminal groups with no questions asked—they're a foundational piece of the ecosystem that enables ransomware operations, phishing platforms, and botnets to survive takedown attempts. Disrupting them, even temporarily, puts friction into criminal operations. Why it matters: both stories underscore that the pace and scale of the security landscape are accelerating—on offense, defense, and in law enforcement alike.

Story five. Wireshark 4.6.6 is out, fixing one security vulnerability along with eleven other bugs. Wireshark is the world's most widely deployed network protocol analyzer, used daily by security analysts, incident responders, and network engineers everywhere. Wireshark vulnerabilities are typically triggered by malicious or specially crafted packet capture files, meaning the risk is highest for analysts who open capture files received from external parties during incident response or threat intelligence sharing workflows. Why it matters: update your Wireshark installation before opening any untrusted capture files. It's a quick update with a real security benefit.
