Good morning. Here is your security digest for May 22, 2026. It has been a busy 24 hours: CISA issued active exploitation warnings, two vendors disclosed maximum-severity vulnerabilities, a DDoS botnet operator was arrested, and a Chinese espionage group was caught using familiar cloud platforms in unfamiliar ways.

Story one. CISA adds actively exploited flaws in Langflow and Trend Micro Apex One to its Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog.

The U.S. Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency placed two vulnerabilities on its must-patch list Thursday. The first is a flaw in Langflow, an open-source tool used by developers to build applications powered by large language models. Attackers are already exploiting this flaw in the wild, and it carries a severity score of 9.4 out of 10. The second is a remote code execution vulnerability in Trend Micro Apex One, a widely deployed endpoint protection platform, rated 9.8 out of 10. An unauthenticated attacker who can reach the Apex One management console can upload and execute arbitrary code on the server. Trend Micro says its cloud-hosted customers are already protected by server-side mitigations, but organizations running Apex One on their own infrastructure need to patch without delay. Why it matters: CISA adding a flaw to its Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog carries regulatory force for federal agencies and acts as an industry-wide patch deadline signal.

Story two. Cisco patches a maximum-severity flaw in Secure Workload.

Cisco has released an urgent fix for its Secure Workload product, a micro-segmentation platform used in large enterprise and government data centers. The vulnerability, tracked as CVE-2026-20223, scores a perfect 10 out of 10. An attacker who can reach the product's API over the network can extract sensitive data without providing any credentials whatsoever. There is no workaround — upgrading to the patched release is the only fix. Secure Workload is specifically designed to enforce east-west traffic controls inside data centers, so a compromise here could undermine broader segmentation strategies meant to contain breaches. If your organization uses Cisco Secure Workload, prioritize this patch today.

Story three. Ubiquiti discloses a cluster of critical vulnerabilities in UniFi OS devices.

Ubiquiti published firmware updates for UniFi OS — the software running on its popular line of routers, switches, and wireless access points — disclosing five CVEs, three of which score the maximum severity of 10 out of 10. The most serious bugs allow a network attacker to make unauthorized changes to the system, traverse the file system to read files that could be used to compromise underlying accounts, or inject and execute arbitrary commands. A fourth vulnerability at 9.1 out of 10 also enables command injection but requires elevated privileges to exploit. UniFi hardware is extremely widespread, spanning small offices, apartment buildings, schools, and enterprise branches. Unpatched devices on exposed management interfaces are at real risk. Log in to your UniFi controller and apply any pending firmware updates.

Story four. Alleged Kimwolf DDoS botnet operator arrested in a joint U.S. and Canada action.

Canadian and U.S. authorities arrested a 23-year-old man from Ottawa named Jacob Butler, who is accused of building and operating the Kimwolf botnet. Kimwolf is assessed to be a variant of a botnet family called AISURU, and at its peak it had infected roughly two million Internet of Things devices — routers, cameras, and similar household hardware — which were then rented out to customers for denial-of-service attacks on demand. Butler, who used the online alias Dort, drew additional public attention earlier this year when the security journalist Brian Krebs named him publicly, after which Butler allegedly launched retaliatory denial-of-service floods, doxing campaigns, and swatting calls against Krebs and a security researcher. He now faces criminal hacking charges in both countries. Why it matters: the takedown removes a significant DDoS-for-hire capability and demonstrates that cross-border law enforcement collaboration continues to produce results against cybercriminal infrastructure operators.

Story five. China-linked Webworm group targets European governments by routing espionage traffic through Discord and Microsoft Graph.

Researchers documented a campaign by a threat group called Webworm, assessed as affiliated with Chinese state interests, that is conducting cyber-espionage against European government organizations. The method is notable: rather than communicating over custom infrastructure that defenders might detect or block, the group is routing command-and-control traffic through Discord and the Microsoft Graph API. Both services are used legitimately in most organizations and are almost never blocked by enterprise firewalls, allowing malicious traffic to blend invisibly with ordinary business communications. The group also employs tunneling software such as SoftEther VPN to add another layer of obfuscation. This follows a pattern, now observed across multiple nation-state groups, of living off trusted cloud services to evade network-level detection. Security teams should consider hunting for outbound traffic to cloud messaging platforms that does not match expected application or user behavior.

That is your digest for May 22, 2026. Immediate patch priorities are Cisco Secure Workload, UniFi OS firmware, and Trend Micro Apex One on-premise deployments. Stay safe out there.
