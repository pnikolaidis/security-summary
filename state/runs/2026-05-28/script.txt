Our lead story is a WordPress plugin vulnerability wave. NVD disclosed ten serious flaws in widely-used plugins on Wednesday, and several of them are about as critical as vulnerabilities get. The most alarming is a flaw in the WPify Woo Czech plugin — scored nine point nine out of ten — that allows an attacker to upload any file type to an affected server. In plain terms, that means dropping a web shell and taking full control of the site. The QuickWebP image optimization plugin and the WebinarIgnition webinar platform each carry their own nine-point-nine path traversal vulnerabilities that let attackers escape their authorized directories and access sensitive files anywhere on the server. WebinarIgnition also has a separate nine-point-eight privilege escalation flaw. The miniOrange OTP Verification plugin carries another nine-point-eight privilege escalation. And five more plugins — including WooCommerce Active Products Tables and MasterStudy LMS — are affected by critical SQL injection flaws, all scoring nine-point-three. If you manage WordPress sites, open your plugin dashboard today and push every available update. The web shell upload vulnerability in particular shouldn't sit unpatched overnight.

Story two: Carnival Cruise Corporation confirmed today that a breach the ShinyHunters extortion gang claimed back in April is real, and it affected nearly six million people. Carnival is the world's largest cruise operator, running brands including Princess Cruises, Holland America, and Cunard. ShinyHunters has hit dozens of major consumer companies over the years, typically threatening to publish stolen data unless they're paid. If you've sailed with any Carnival-family brand, watch for phishing attempts that mention your travel history or booking details. That kind of personalized context is exactly how criminals make follow-on attacks more convincing.

Third, some good news. CrowdStrike, Google, and the Shadowserver Foundation jointly took down GlassWorm, a supply-chain botnet that's been targeting software developers since at least early 2025. GlassWorm operators distributed malicious packages and IDE extensions, then routed their commands through Solana blockchain transactions and the BitTorrent peer-to-peer network — two systems that were specifically chosen because you can't take them down by seizing a few servers the way you can with traditional infrastructure. The fact that a coordinated three-party coalition managed to cut all channels simultaneously is a real win. Developers should audit their installed packages and IDE extensions, particularly anything added in 2025 from less familiar sources, for signs of compromise.

Story four introduces JINX zero one sixty four — a threat actor Wiz researchers are naming publicly for the first time. This group is going after cryptocurrency firms with the goal of stealing digital assets, and their approach is more sophisticated than typical crypto theft. They pose as recruiters to lure technical employees into running custom macOS malware, but the real target isn't just the endpoint. The malware specifically goes after CI/CD infrastructure — the build pipelines where code gets compiled, signed, and deployed. Getting into a build pipeline gives an attacker access to code signing keys, deployment credentials, and potentially the ability to slip malicious code into a legitimate software release. Crypto firms should treat unsolicited recruiter outreach with heightened suspicion and double-check who has access to their build systems.

And finally, a cryptojacking campaign is taking an unusual approach to finding victims. Rather than relying on phishing emails or compromised websites, this group is poisoning both search engine results and AI chatbot responses to direct people toward malware downloads. Someone searching for tools for high-performance GPU work — or asking an AI assistant for software recommendations — gets steered toward a malicious installer that quietly runs mining software in the background. This is one of the first campaigns to meaningfully exploit AI assistants as a distribution vector. If your team uses AI tools to discover and install software, it's worth adding a manual verification step before running anything the chatbot suggests.
